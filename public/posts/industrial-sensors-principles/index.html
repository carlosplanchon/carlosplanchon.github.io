Principles of Operation of Industrial Sensors

An overview of the fundamental principles behind common industrial sensors used in automation and process control.

Industrial sensors are the foundation of modern automation and process control. They convert physical phenomena into electrical signals that can be measured, monitored, and used to control systems. This post explores the operating principles of the most common sensor types.

Temperature Sensors

Thermocouples

Based on the Seebeck effect: when two dissimilar metals are joined, a voltage proportional to temperature difference develops between the junction and the open ends. They offer wide temperature range (-200°C to 2300°C) but lower accuracy.

Resistance Temperature Detectors (RTDs)

Utilize the predictable resistance change of pure metals (typically platinum) with temperature. Formula: R(T) = R₀(1 + αT). Higher accuracy and stability than thermocouples, but narrower range and slower response.

Thermistors

Semiconductor devices with high temperature coefficient of resistance. Negative Temperature Coefficient (NTC) thermistors decrease resistance with rising temperature. High sensitivity in narrow ranges, commonly used in protection circuits.

Position and Proximity Sensors

Inductive Sensors

Use a coil to generate an alternating magnetic field. When a conductive target approaches, eddy currents form in the target, reducing the coil’s impedance. Detection range depends on target conductivity and sensor size.

Capacitive Sensors

Detect changes in capacitance between the sensor electrode and target. The capacitor is part of an RC oscillator; presence of any material (conductive or dielectric) changes the dielectric constant and oscillation frequency. Can detect metals, plastics, and liquids.

Photoelectric Sensors

Operate on light transmission or reflection:

  • Through-beam: Emitter and receiver on opposite sides
  • Retroreflective: Emitter and receiver in one unit, reflector opposite
  • Diffuse: Emitter and receiver in one unit, detects reflected light

Pressure Sensors

Piezoresistive Sensors

Utilize the resistance change of semiconductor materials under strain. A diaphragm deflects under pressure, stretching piezoresistors and changing their resistance. High sensitivity, suitable for low-pressure applications.

Capacitive Pressure Sensors

Measure deflection of a diaphragm by changes in capacitance between the diaphragm and a fixed electrode. Excellent for very low pressures and vacuum applications.

Piezoelectric Sensors

Generate charge when certain crystals (quartz, PZT) undergo mechanical stress. Ideal for dynamic pressure measurements (vibrations, shocks) but not for static measurements.

Flow Sensors

Differential Pressure Flowmeters

Based on Bernoulli’s principle: flow rate is proportional to the square root of pressure drop across a restriction (orifice plate, Venturi tube). Simple, well-understood, but introduces pressure loss.

Magnetic Flowmeters

Faraday’s law: conductor moving through magnetic field induces voltage proportional to velocity. Requires conductive fluid. No pressure drop, suitable for slurries and corrosive liquids.

Ultrasonic Flowmeters

Measure time difference between upstream and downstream sound pulses (time-of-flight) or Doppler shift from reflections. Non-invasive, no pressure drop, but limited to clean fluids.

Coriolis Flowmeters

Measure mass flow directly using Coriolis forces on vibrating tubes. Most accurate type, but expensive and introduces pressure drop.

Level Sensors

Ultrasonic Level Sensors

Send ultrasonic pulses, measure time to reflection from surface. Non-contact, works with solids and liquids. Affected by foam, vapors, and dust.

Capacitive Level Sensors

Detect level changes through capacitance between electrodes. Can be installed externally (clamped to tank) or internally. Suitable for conductive and non-conductive materials.

Float-type Level Sensors

Buoyant element rises with liquid level, mechanically linked to indicator or switch. Simple, reliable, but moving parts can wear.

Force and Load Sensors

Strain Gauge Load Cells

Four strain gauges in a Wheatstone bridge configuration. Under load, gauges stretch/compress, changing resistance. Output is proportional to force applied. Common types: shear beam, single-point, button.

Conclusion

Understanding sensor principles enables proper selection, installation, and troubleshooting. Key considerations include:

  • Measurement range and accuracy required
  • Material compatibility
  • Environment (temperature, humidity, vibrations)
  • Response time needs
  • Output signal type (analog, digital, networked)

Each sensor type has trade-offs; the optimal choice depends on the specific application requirements.
