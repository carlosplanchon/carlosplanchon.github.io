Aerodynamics: Lift and Drag

Understanding the fundamental aerodynamic forces that enable aircraft to fly.

Aerodynamics studies how air flows around bodies and the forces generated. For aircraft, the two primary aerodynamic forces are lift and drag.

Lift

Lift is the force perpendicular to the relative wind, counteracting weight. It is generated by the wing’s airfoil shape and the principle of Bernoulli:

L = ½ × ρ × V² × S × Cₗ

Where ρ is air density, V is velocity, S is wing area, and Cₗ is the lift coefficient.

How Lift Is Generated

An airfoil accelerates air over its upper surface, decreasing pressure above (creating suction) while higher pressure below pushes upward. This pressure differential, combined with the deflection of air downward, produces lift.

The angle of attack (AOA) critically affects lift: increasing AOA increases Cₗ up to the stall angle (typically 15-20°), where airflow separates and lift drops dramatically.

Drag

Drag opposes forward motion. It comes in several forms:

Parasite Drag

  • Form drag: Caused by the shape of the body disrupting airflow
  • Skin friction drag: Viscous friction between air and the surface
  • Interference drag: Created when airflow around different components interacts

Induced Drag

A consequence of lift generation. To produce lift, wings create a pressure difference that causes air to curl from the high-pressure bottom to the low-pressure top, forming wingtip vortices. These vortices induce a downward component to the velocity, requiring additional thrust to overcome.

Wave Drag

At transonic and supersonic speeds, shock waves form, dramatically increasing drag.

The Lift-to-Drag Ratio

The ratio L/D indicates aerodynamic efficiency. Gliders achieve 50:1 or higher; typical airliners cruise at around 15:1. Maximizing L/D minimizes fuel consumption.

Understanding lift and drag is fundamental to aircraft design, performance analysis, and flight operations.
